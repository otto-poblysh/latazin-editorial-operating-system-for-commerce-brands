# House Voice

## Purpose

This file defines the character of the Latazin editorial voice — how it sounds, what intelligence it projects, what restraint it maintains, and how it differs from generic e-commerce copy.

The voice is not a personality. It is a set of commitments about how the system communicates: what it values in language, what it refuses to do, and what kind of relationship it builds with the reader.

This file exists to:

- Establish a consistent editorial character across all generated content
- Prevent the system from defaulting to promotional, generic, or hype-driven language
- Give writers, reviewers, and prompts a shared standard for how the writing should sound
- Distinguish Latazin editorial from conventional e-commerce copy at every level

---

## How this file is used in the system

- **During generation:** The latazin content generation prompt ingests this file to shape the tone, register, and posture of all drafted copy. Every generated latazin should be audibly consistent with this voice.
- **During review:** Reviewers and the quality rubric reference this file to evaluate whether generated content sounds like the system or has drifted toward generic copy. Voice consistency is a pass/fail criterion.
- **During planning:** Annual, quarterly, and monthly planning prompts reference the voice to ensure that strategic framing, calendar language, and campaign descriptions maintain the editorial register.
- **During prompt design:** When new prompts are written or existing prompts are refined, this file serves as the voice constraint. A prompt that cannot produce output consistent with this voice needs to be revised.

## Voice Character Definition

### What the voice is

The Latazin voice is **composed, specific, and materially grounded.** It speaks with the confidence of someone who has looked closely, chosen deliberately, and formed a clear opinion — but does not need to announce that confidence.

It is:

- **Declarative without being loud.** Sentences state positions. They do not hedge, apologize, or oversell. The voice says what it means and moves on.
- **Specific without being exhaustive.** Details are chosen for effect, not completeness. A good sentence picks one material fact, one visual detail, or one cultural reference — not all of them.
- **Warm without being familiar.** The voice respects the reader but does not perform intimacy. It does not say "we" conspiratorially or "you" accusingly. It addresses an intelligent adult.
- **Opinionated without being aggressive.** The voice has preferences and says so. It believes certain things are better than others. But it persuades through demonstration, not insistence.
- **Edited.** The voice sounds like someone removed the unnecessary words. Sentences are not short for the sake of brevity — they are precise because someone cared enough to cut.

### What the voice is not

- Not breathless or excitable
- Not detached or academic
- Not folksy or chatty
- Not corporate or institutional
- Not provocative for its own sake
- Not minimalist to the point of emptiness
- Not maximalist to the point of noise

### The voice in one sentence

The Latazin voice sounds like an editor who sells — someone who knows the product, respects the reader, and believes that good writing is the best sales tool a commerce brand has.

## Intelligence and Restraint

### The intelligence the voice projects

The Latazin voice should make the reader feel that the brand:

1. **Knows its products deeply.** Not just names and prices, but materials, construction, how they feel, how they age, what they pair with, and what they replace.

2. **Understands its context.** The voice knows what season it is, what occasions matter, what the culture is paying attention to, and where the product fits in the reader's life.

3. **Has editorial judgment.** The voice selects, sequences, and structures. It does not dump information. It decides what to say first, what to save, and what to leave out entirely.

4. **Reads widely.** The voice draws on references from culture, design, food, architecture, literature, and urban life — but only when those references genuinely illuminate the product. References are never decorative.

5. **Knows the difference between information and meaning.** The voice can describe a product factually. But it chooses to describe the product meaningfully — connecting features to experiences, materials to feelings, prices to values.

### The restraint the voice maintains

1. **It does not oversell.** If the product is good, the writing shows why. It does not stack adjectives, inflate claims, or manufacture urgency. A product that needs to be screamed about is a product the voice does not believe in.

2. **It does not over-explain.** The reader is assumed to be intelligent, visually literate, and capable of drawing conclusions. The voice provides premises and evidence, not hand-held explanations.

3. **It does not perform emotion.** The voice does not tell the reader how to feel. It does not say "you'll love this" or "we're obsessed." It creates conditions under which the reader arrives at their own feeling.

4. **It does not chase trends.** The voice is aware of what is current but does not adopt trending language, formats, or postures. It does not sound like this week's social media. It sounds like something that would still sound right in six months.

5. **It does not fill silence.** Not every page needs copy. Not every product needs a paragraph. The voice knows when an image is enough, when white space is more persuasive than words, and when the reader needs a pause.

## How Latazin Differs from Generic E-Commerce Copy

| Dimension | Generic e-commerce copy | Latazin editorial voice |
|---|---|---|
| **Default mode** | Promotional | Editorial |
| **Relationship to reader** | Buyer to be converted | Reader to be persuaded |
| **Product language** | Feature list with adjectives | Material, contextual, and experiential description |
| **Urgency** | Manufactured ("Don't miss out!") | Structural (timing tied to season, occasion, or collection logic) |
| **CTAs** | Frequent, loud, repeated | Sparse, earned, placed with intent |
| **Tone** | Excited, breathless, casual | Composed, warm, specific |
| **Cultural references** | Trending hashtags and memes | Architecture, food, cities, literature, material culture |
| **Emotion** | Told ("You'll love this!") | Created through detail and pacing |
| **Structure** | List of products with captions | Sequenced argument with rhythm and rest |
| **Trust mechanism** | Social proof, reviews, badges | Editorial authority, material specificity, taste demonstration |
| **Adjective use** | Stacked for intensity | Selected for precision |
| **Paragraph length** | Short for scanning | Varied for rhythm |
| **Brand personality** | Performative friendliness | Earned respect |

## Voice Do's and Don'ts

### Do

- **Do open with a premise, not a product.** The first sentence should establish why the reader is here — a question, an observation, a seasonal truth, a cultural reference. The product earns its appearance through context.
- **Do use concrete details.** Name the material. Describe the weight. Reference the city. Mention the season. Specificity is the foundation of trust.
- **Do let structure persuade.** A well-sequenced latazin — premise, evidence, pause, product, invitation — is more persuasive than any single brilliant sentence.
- **Do vary sentence length.** A long sentence that builds a scene followed by a short sentence that lands a point. Rhythm is not optional.
- **Do trust the image.** When a photograph says everything, the copy should say almost nothing. A caption can be one line. A page can be wordless.
- **Do write for rereading.** The best editorial copy holds up on second reading. It reveals more, not less.
- **Do name what the product replaces.** What was the reader using, wearing, or living with before? What changes? This grounds the product in real life.
- **Do connect the product to a world.** A bag is not just a bag — it belongs to a city, a season, a way of moving through public space. Place the product in its world.

### Don't

- **Don't open with the product name.** Earn the product reveal. Context first, product second.
- **Don't use superlatives without evidence.** "The finest leather" means nothing without naming the tannery, the hide, or the finish. Unsupported superlatives are noise.
- **Don't address the reader as if they are a child.** No "Guess what?" No "Ready for this?" No rhetorical questions that assume the reader cannot think for themselves.
- **Don't stack adjectives.** "Luxurious, buttery-soft, premium Italian leather" is three adjectives doing the work one good sentence could do. Pick the detail that matters most.
- **Don't use filler transitions.** "Speaking of which," "And that's not all," "But wait" — these are the scaffolding of lazy copy. Remove them.
- **Don't manufacture urgency.** "Limited time only!" and "Almost gone!" are promotional tactics, not editorial language. If the product is genuinely limited, state the fact plainly.
- **Don't use exclamation marks in body copy.** One per latazin at most, and only if the sentence genuinely warrants it. Excitement is demonstrated through content, not punctuation.
- **Don't write CTAs that shout.** "SHOP NOW" and "BUY TODAY" belong to banner ads, not editorial commerce. A Latazin CTA should feel like a natural next step: "See the collection," "View the piece," "Explore the full range."
- **Don't mimic social media language.** "Obsessed," "iconic," "main character energy," "that girl" — these phrases have a shelf life of weeks. The voice should outlast them.
- **Don't explain the brand's values in every piece.** The voice demonstrates values through how it writes, what it chooses to describe, and what it omits. Stating values explicitly is a sign that the writing is not doing its job.

## Tone Calibration Guidance

The Latazin voice is consistent in character but flexible in tone. Different content types, product categories, and occasions call for tonal adjustments within the same voice.

### Tone spectrum

The voice operates along a spectrum from **restrained** to **inviting.** It never reaches either extreme — it is never cold, and it is never gushing.

```
Cold ← — — Restrained — Composed — Warm — Inviting — — → Gushing
              ▲                                ▲
              │         OPERATING RANGE         │
              └────────────────────────────────-┘
```

### Calibration by content type

| Content type | Tone position | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Product argument | Composed to warm | Lead with material and contextual detail. Confident but not aggressive. |
| Collection / drop | Warm to inviting | The reader is being welcomed into a world. Slightly more generous in language. |
| Craft study | Restrained to composed | Let the process speak. Minimal editorializing. Respect the maker. |
| Occasion | Warm to inviting | The reader is preparing for something. The voice should feel like a thoughtful friend, not a salesperson. |
| Manifesto | Composed | Declarative. Clear. Every sentence should feel final and intentional. |
| Misreading correction | Restrained to composed | The voice is correcting a misconception. Calm authority. No defensiveness. |
| Collaboration | Warm | Two voices meeting. The tone should acknowledge both parties without losing the house voice. |
| Milestone / proof | Composed to warm | Confidence grounded in evidence. Not boastful. |
| Calendar marking | Warm to inviting | Seasonal, occasional. The voice marks time with specificity and care. |
| Bundle / world | Warm | Products are presented as a coherent set. The tone should feel like a guided tour, not a catalogue. |

### Calibration by audience proximity

| Audience | Tone adjustment |
|---|---|
| New reader (discovery) | Slightly more inviting. More context. Fewer assumptions about familiarity. |
| Returning reader (loyalty) | Slightly more composed. More shorthand. Acknowledge shared taste without being clubby. |
| Gift buyer (proxy) | Warm and confident. The voice is helping someone make a decision for someone else. Specificity matters more than mood. |
| Professional buyer (wholesale) | Composed and direct. Material facts, positioning clarity, margin for interpretation. Less atmosphere, more precision. |

### Calibration by price point

| Price position | Tone adjustment |
|---|---|
| Accessible | Warm. Emphasize value, versatility, and daily usefulness. Do not apologize for price or overcompensate with aspiration. |
| Mid-range | Composed. Emphasize quality, material, and thoughtfulness. The product should feel considered, not cheap or expensive. |
| Premium | Restrained to composed. Let the product, material, and craft carry the argument. Less language, more precision. Do not inflate. |

### Calibration by season

| Season | Tone adjustment |
|---|---|
| Spring | Warm, forward-looking. Language of return, renewal, and intention — but not cliché. |
| Summer | Inviting, sensory. Language of light, heat, travel, and ease — but grounded in specific products and places. |
| Autumn | Composed, reflective. Language of transition, layering, and preparation — but not melancholy. |
| Winter | Warm to restrained, depending on context. Gifting content is warm. January content is composed and fresh. |

## Voice Consistency Checks

When reviewing content against this voice definition, ask:

1. **Could this paragraph have been written by any brand?** If yes, it fails. The voice should feel specific to a system that values taste, structure, and material intelligence.
2. **Does this sentence earn its adjectives?** Every modifier should add information, not just intensity.
3. **Is the reader being told how to feel, or given reason to feel?** The voice creates conditions; it does not dictate reactions.
4. **Would this sentence sound right if read aloud in a quiet room?** If it needs exclamation or performance to work, it is not in voice.
5. **Does the pacing feel edited?** Sentences should vary in length. Paragraphs should vary in density. The reader should feel rhythm, not monotony.
6. **Is the product placed in a world, or floating in a void?** The voice anchors products in seasons, cities, occasions, materials, and cultural life.

## Maintenance Notes

This file should be reviewed when:

- Generated content consistently drifts from the defined voice character
- New content types or latazin types are added to the system
- Audience definitions change significantly
- New product categories require tonal recalibration
- Reviewers disagree on whether specific content is "in voice"

Changes to this file should be conservative. The voice is the system's editorial identity. Frequent changes create inconsistency. Adjustments should be made only when repeated evidence suggests the current definition is incomplete, unclear, or producing the wrong output.
