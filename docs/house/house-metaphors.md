# House Metaphors

## Purpose

This file defines the metaphor families that the Latazin editorial system draws from when generating, reviewing, or refining content.

Metaphors shape how readers understand products, collections, and brand worlds. The wrong metaphor makes a brand sound generic, performative, or detached from commerce. The right metaphor makes a product feel inevitable — rooted in taste, culture, material reality, and public life.

This file exists to:

- Prevent lazy, generic, or mismatched figurative language
- Give writers and prompts a shared vocabulary of approved metaphor domains
- Ensure that editorial content stays grounded in the world of commerce, craft, and culture
- Provide a screening tool for reviewing generated copy

---

## How this file is used in the system

- **During generation:** Prompts reference this file to constrain the metaphor palette available to the language model. When a latazin is being drafted, the generation prompt should prefer metaphors from these families and avoid those listed in the anti-patterns section.
- **During review:** The quality rubric references this file when evaluating whether a latazin's language feels editorial and taste-forward. Reviewers check generated copy against these families to confirm the figurative register is appropriate.
- **During planning:** Strategy and calendar prompts may draw on metaphor families to frame quarterly themes, seasonal arcs, and campaign language.
- **During style enforcement:** The style guide works alongside this file. Where the style guide governs sentence-level mechanics, this file governs the conceptual register of the imagery.

## Metaphor Families

### 1. Commerce and Exchange

**Domain:** The language of markets, transactions, value, currency, trade, and economic life.

**Core metaphors and examples:**

- A product "earns its place" in the wardrobe
- A collection "trades" on a specific aesthetic currency
- A latazin "offers terms" to the reader
- A price "reflects a position," not just a cost
- The brand "invests" in a material, a silhouette, or a relationship
- A purchase is "an exchange of trust," not just money for goods
- A product "holds its value" — materially, aesthetically, or culturally

**When to use:**

- When discussing price, value, or positioning
- When framing a product as a considered decision rather than an impulse
- When arguing that a product is worth its price through craft, material, or meaning
- When establishing the brand's relationship with its audience as a fair exchange

**What to avoid:**

- Making commerce metaphors sound transactional or cold
- Using financial jargon ("ROI," "leverage," "optimize") as if writing a business plan
- Reducing value to price alone — the metaphor should elevate the exchange, not flatten it

---

### 2. Taste and Judgment

**Domain:** The language of discernment, preference, curation, selection, and aesthetic confidence.

**Core metaphors and examples:**

- A product "rewards close looking"
- A collection "makes a case" for a specific way of dressing
- A brand "exercises judgment" in what it chooses to produce and omit
- The editorial voice "trusts the reader's eye"
- A latazin "sharpens" or "refines" the reader's sense of what matters
- A color, texture, or silhouette is "a declaration of preference"
- An edit is "what remains after the unnecessary has been removed"

**When to use:**

- When positioning the brand as having a clear point of view
- When describing why certain products were selected, paired, or sequenced
- When differentiating from brands that try to please everyone
- When the content needs to convey authority without arrogance

**What to avoid:**

- Taste language that sounds elitist, exclusionary, or condescending
- Calling things "curated" without specifying the logic of curation
- Claiming taste without demonstrating it through specifics

---

### 3. Sequence and Rhythm

**Domain:** The language of order, pacing, progression, timing, and editorial structure.

**Core metaphors and examples:**

- A latazin "opens with a premise and closes with an invitation"
- A content calendar "builds rhythm across the month"
- A quarterly plan "creates an arc, not a pile"
- Products are "introduced in sequence, not dumped in bulk"
- A launch is "timed," not just "scheduled"
- The editorial voice "knows when to pause"
- A page "breathes" between argument and image

**When to use:**

- When discussing editorial planning, calendars, or content cadence
- When structuring a latazin's page flow
- When arguing for restraint or pacing in how products are revealed
- When framing the relationship between content pieces across time

**What to avoid:**

- Rhythm language that sounds mechanical ("cadence optimization," "content velocity")
- Implying that more frequent publishing is inherently better
- Treating sequence as a scheduling tactic rather than an editorial choice

---

### 4. Ritual and Ceremony

**Domain:** The language of occasions, marking, preparation, and the formality of everyday life.

**Core metaphors and examples:**

- Getting dressed is "a daily act of composition"
- A gift is "a gesture with weight"
- A seasonal shift is "a reason to reconsider what you carry"
- A product "marks an occasion" — a dinner, a trip, a first day
- Unboxing is "a small ceremony of arrival"
- A capsule wardrobe is "a set of agreements with yourself"
- A purchase "commemorates a decision"

**When to use:**

- When writing about gifting, occasions, celebrations, or seasonal transitions
- When elevating a product above the functional into the meaningful
- When connecting a product to a moment, not just a need
- When the content wants to slow the reader down and invite attention

**What to avoid:**

- Making ritual language sound pretentious or self-important
- Over-sacralizing everyday products — a t-shirt is not a religious artifact
- Using ceremony language to disguise thin product meaning
- Treating every product as if it deserves a ritual framing

---

### 5. Materiality and Craft

**Domain:** The language of making, materials, process, hand, finish, and physical reality.

**Core metaphors and examples:**

- A fabric "falls" a certain way; it does not just "look nice"
- A stitch pattern "tells you something about how the garment was thought through"
- A finish is "the last decision the maker made before release"
- A material "ages," "softens," "patinas" — it has a future, not just a present
- Craft is "visible attention"
- A product is "built" or "constructed," not just "designed"
- Weight, drape, texture, and hand are "the product's physical argument"

**When to use:**

- When describing product quality, materials, or construction
- When differentiating a product from mass-market alternatives
- When making a case for price through process
- When the reader should feel the product through words before they touch it

**What to avoid:**

- Craft language used decoratively without substance ("artisan," "handcrafted") when the product does not support the claim
- Fetishizing craft for its own sake without connecting it to the buyer's experience
- Using materiality metaphors for digital or abstract topics where they feel forced

---

### 6. Perception and Atmosphere

**Domain:** The language of how things feel, the mood they create, and the sensory world they belong to.

**Core metaphors and examples:**

- A color palette "sets a temperature"
- A product "belongs in a specific light"
- A collection "creates a room you want to enter"
- A fragrance "occupies the space between intention and memory"
- The editorial voice "adjusts the lighting"
- A latazin should "feel like arriving somewhere specific"
- A photograph "holds a mood, not just a product"

**When to use:**

- When establishing the emotional register of a latazin or campaign
- When writing about products that rely on sensory experience (fragrance, texture, color)
- When setting the scene for a collection or seasonal story
- When the goal is immersion, not just information

**What to avoid:**

- Vague atmosphere language that says nothing specific ("vibes," "aesthetic," "mood")
- Synesthesia that confuses rather than clarifies
- Atmosphere without substance — mood is not a substitute for meaning
- Describing feelings the product cannot actually deliver

---

### 7. Public Meaning and Culture

**Domain:** The language of how products exist in the world — in cities, in streets, in cultural life, in public conversation.

**Core metaphors and examples:**

- A product "enters a conversation" that already exists
- A brand "takes a position" in the culture, not just the market
- A collection "responds to how people actually live"
- A garment is "a public object" — it exists in the world, on streets, in rooms
- A brand's editorial voice is "a contribution to public taste"
- A latazin "participates in culture, not just commerce"
- A product is "legible" — people can read what it means

**When to use:**

- When connecting a product to a broader cultural moment or reference
- When positioning the brand's worldview
- When arguing that a product matters beyond its functional use
- When writing about collaborations, community, milestones, or public life
- When the content needs to feel relevant, not just beautiful

**What to avoid:**

- Cultural references that are forced, outdated, or appropriative
- Claiming cultural significance the brand has not earned
- Using public meaning language to obscure a lack of product substance
- Name-dropping cultural references for credibility rather than genuine connection

## Anti-Patterns: Metaphors to Avoid

The following metaphor habits are signs of weak, generic, or misaligned editorial work. Content using these patterns should be flagged during review.

### Generic intensity language

- "Elevate your style"
- "Take it to the next level"
- "Unlock your potential"
- "Transform your look"

**Why it fails:** These phrases are empty escalation. They promise a change in degree without specifying what changes or why. They belong to fitness ads and SaaS landing pages, not editorial commerce.

### Journey and adventure metaphors

- "Embark on a journey"
- "Your style adventure"
- "The path to self-expression"

**Why it fails:** Journey metaphors are overused across every industry. They imply movement without direction and discovery without specificity. They flatten the difference between buying a jacket and backpacking through Patagonia.

### War and conquest metaphors

- "Conquer the season"
- "Arm yourself with style"
- "Slay your look"

**Why it fails:** Aggressive language contradicts the restraint, taste, and intelligence that Latazin editorial should project. Commerce is an exchange, not a battle.

### Royalty and luxury clichés

- "Treat yourself like royalty"
- "Fit for a king/queen"
- "The crown jewel of the collection"

**Why it fails:** These metaphors are borrowed prestige. They substitute inherited status for earned taste. They sound like hotel marketing, not editorial commerce.

### Tech and startup language

- "Disrupt your wardrobe"
- "Optimize your style"
- "Hack your morning routine"
- "Leverage this collection"

**Why it fails:** Tech metaphors treat products as systems to be optimized rather than objects to be appreciated. They prioritize efficiency over meaning and strip away the sensory, cultural, and material dimensions of commerce.

### Vague emotional inflation

- "Fall in love with..."
- "Obsessed with this piece"
- "You NEED this"
- "We're dying over..."

**Why it fails:** Emotional inflation is the fastest way to sound like every other brand on social media. It substitutes exclamation for argument and hype for judgment. The reader is told how to feel rather than given reasons to feel it.

### Nature metaphors used decoratively

- "Inspired by the ocean"
- "As free as the wind"
- "Like a breath of fresh air"

**Why it fails:** Nature metaphors work only when materially grounded — a linen that breathes in heat, a palette drawn from coastal stone. Used loosely, they are decorative filler that connects to nothing specific about the product.

## Maintenance Notes

This file should be reviewed when:

- New latazin types are added to the typology
- Generated content consistently drifts toward unapproved metaphor families
- A new product category enters the system that requires a new metaphor domain
- Reviewers notice recurring figurative patterns not covered here

Changes to this file should be deliberate. The metaphor palette is part of the system's editorial identity, and expanding it without care dilutes the voice.
