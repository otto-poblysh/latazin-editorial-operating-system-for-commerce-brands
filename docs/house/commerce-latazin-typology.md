# Commerce Latazin Typology

## Purpose

This file defines every type of latazin the system can produce. A latazin type is not a template — it is a structural intent. It determines what the latazin argues, how it is sequenced, which page roles it uses, and what the reader should understand or feel by the end. Without a clear type, a latazin defaults to a decorated product listing — the most common failure in commerce content.

The typology exists to ensure that every latazin has a reason for existing that goes beyond "we have a product to sell." Each type answers a different editorial question, serves a different strategic purpose, and places products in a different kind of meaning.

This is one of the most important files in the system. It governs what gets made.

---

## How This File Is Used in the System

- **During generation:** The model receives a latazin type as part of the brief. It uses this file to understand what that type requires structurally, tonally, and argumentatively. The type determines which page roles are mandatory, how the product appears, and what the opening logic should be.
- **During review:** Reviewers check whether the generated latazin fulfils the requirements of its declared type. A latazin that declares itself a Craft Study but reads like a Product Argument is a type failure, regardless of writing quality.
- **During planning:** Monthly and quarterly planning documents use the typology to ensure variety across the editorial calendar. A month of nothing but Product Arguments is a planning failure. The typology provides the vocabulary for diverse editorial programming.
- **During prompt design:** New prompts specify type requirements drawn from this file, ensuring that structural expectations are embedded in the generation process.

## Complete Typology

### 1. Product Argument

**What it does:** Makes a case for a single product. Not a product description — an argument. It establishes why this product matters, what it replaces, where it belongs in the reader's life, and why now is the time.

**When to use it:**
- A product has a genuine story beyond its features
- The product solves a problem the reader may not have articulated
- The product represents a category position worth arguing (e.g., "the only bag you need for summer")
- A hero product needs editorial support for a launch or relaunch

**What it is best for:**
- Converting editorial attention into product understanding
- Positioning a single product as the answer to a specific question
- Building product authority through context, detail, and restrained persuasion

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The product has no distinguishing argument beyond "it's nice" or "it's new"
- The latazin lists features instead of building a case
- Multiple products compete for attention within a single Product Argument
- The argument is generic ("quality materials, timeless design") rather than specific

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Bundle/World**, it focuses on one product, not a set or ecosystem
- Unlike a **Craft Study**, it argues for the product's place in the reader's life, not the process behind it
- Unlike a **Shop the Look**, it builds a sustained argument rather than presenting styled combinations

---

### 2. Bundle/World

**What it does:** Presents a group of products as a coherent world. The products are not merely listed together — they are shown to belong together. The latazin builds an environment, a logic of combination, a reason why these items share a page.

**When to use it:**
- Multiple products share an aesthetic, material, occasion, or philosophy
- The brand wants to encourage multi-product discovery without hard cross-selling
- A seasonal shift creates a natural grouping ("everything you need for the table this autumn")
- The editorial goal is atmosphere and world-building, not individual product persuasion

**What it is best for:**
- Increasing average order awareness through editorial context
- Introducing a brand's range to new readers through a curated lens
- Creating mood and lifestyle context that individual product pages cannot

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The products are grouped only by price point or newness, not by editorial logic
- The "world" is a thin justification for a product dump
- No atmosphere is built — the latazin reads like a catalogue page with prose decoration
- The connections between products are stated but not shown

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Collection/Drop**, a Bundle/World is editorially assembled, not brand-defined. The grouping is the editor's argument, not the brand's release schedule
- Unlike a **Shop the Look**, it builds a world with narrative and atmosphere, not a visual styling guide
- Unlike a **Product Argument**, it does not focus on a single product's case but on the logic of combination

---

### 3. Craft Study

**What it does:** Examines how something is made. It goes behind the product to the process, the material decisions, the maker's intent, the trade-offs, and the skill. A Craft Study earns the reader's respect for the product through demonstrated knowledge, not claimed quality.

**When to use it:**
- The production process is genuinely interesting or unusual
- Material choices involve meaningful trade-offs worth explaining
- A maker, artisan, or production facility has a story that illuminates the product
- The brand's manufacturing approach is a key differentiator

**What it is best for:**
- Building brand credibility through transparency and specificity
- Justifying premium pricing through demonstrated process
- Attracting readers who value how things are made, not just what they look like
- Creating content with long shelf life — craft doesn't expire

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The craft story is generic ("handmade with care by skilled artisans") rather than specific
- The process described is standard for the category and not genuinely differentiating
- The latazin becomes a factory tour with no editorial angle
- Technical details are included without translation into reader-relevant meaning

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Product Argument**, the focus is on process, not positioning
- Unlike a **Manifesto**, it shows rather than declares — the craft is the evidence, not a philosophical stance
- Unlike a **Collaboration**, the focus is on internal craft, not external partnership

---

### 4. Occasion

**What it does:** Ties products to a moment in time — a holiday, a season, a life event, a cultural moment, a weekly rhythm. The occasion provides the reason for the latazin's existence and the context for product relevance.

**When to use it:**
- A calendar moment creates natural product relevance (gifting season, back-to-school, summer travel)
- A cultural or seasonal shift changes what products mean or how they are used
- The brand wants to position itself within a reader's life rhythm, not just their shopping list
- Timeliness is the editorial strategy — this content matters now, specifically

**What it is best for:**
- Creating urgency through calendar context rather than manufactured scarcity
- Connecting products to the reader's actual life moments
- Building a publishing rhythm that readers anticipate and return for
- Giving products temporal meaning ("the coat for this November, not coats in general")

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The occasion is a thin pretext for a product listing ("Valentine's Day gifts" with no editorial angle)
- The latazin could be published at any time — the occasion is decorative, not structural
- Generic seasonal copy replaces specific cultural observation
- The occasion overwhelms the product — all festivity, no substance

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Calendar Marking**, an Occasion latazin is product-forward, using the moment to frame commerce. A Calendar Marking may have minimal or no product content
- Unlike a **Product Argument**, the occasion, not the product, is the premise
- Unlike a **Bundle/World**, the grouping logic is temporal, not aesthetic

---

### 5. Misreading Correction

**What it does:** Addresses a common misunderstanding, assumption, or cliché about a product, category, or material and corrects it. The latazin identifies what people get wrong and uses that as the entry point for a more nuanced understanding.

**When to use it:**
- A product or category suffers from persistent misconceptions
- The brand's price point, material choice, or design approach is frequently misunderstood
- Reader objections or hesitations are known and can be addressed editorially
- A product category has been cheapened by market trends and deserves reconsideration

**What it is best for:**
- Overcoming purchase hesitation through education, not persuasion
- Positioning the brand as more knowledgeable than the market
- Creating content that feels genuinely useful — the reader learns something
- Addressing negative perceptions without being defensive

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The "misreading" is invented to justify the latazin — no one actually holds the misconception
- The correction is condescending ("Most people think X, but actually...")
- The latazin becomes a defensive rebuttal rather than an editorial exploration
- The corrected understanding doesn't connect back to a specific product or purchase decision

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Product Argument**, the entry point is the misconception, not the product
- Unlike a **Manifesto**, the tone is educational, not declarative — it corrects, it doesn't proclaim
- Unlike a **Craft Study**, the focus is on the reader's understanding, not the maker's process

---

### 6. Manifesto

**What it does:** States a position. A Manifesto latazin declares what the brand believes about commerce, taste, quality, design, or culture. It is not a mission statement — it is a published editorial stance that earns the reader's alignment through clarity of thought, not emotional appeal.

**When to use it:**
- The brand needs to articulate a position that distinguishes it from competitors
- A cultural moment invites a clear, substantive response (not a reactive hot take)
- The brand is entering a new category and needs to establish its editorial authority
- The editorial calendar needs a non-product piece that reinforces brand identity

**What it is best for:**
- Building brand authority and editorial identity over time
- Attracting readers who share the brand's values and taste
- Creating cornerstone content that other latazins can reference
- Establishing a framework that gives future product arguments more weight

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The manifesto is a collection of platitudes ("We believe in quality, sustainability, and you")
- The position is uncontroversial to the point of meaninglessness
- The latazin is a thinly disguised "About Us" page
- The language is grandiose but the ideas are generic

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Product Argument**, a Manifesto may contain no product at all — the argument is about ideas
- Unlike a **Craft Study**, the focus is on philosophy, not process
- Unlike a **Misreading Correction**, the tone is declarative, not corrective
- Unlike a **Milestone/Proof**, the authority comes from the argument itself, not from evidence of past success

---

### 7. Collection/Drop

**What it does:** Introduces a brand-defined collection or product release. Unlike a Bundle/World (which is editorially assembled), a Collection/Drop latazin works with a grouping the brand has already created. The editorial task is to give that collection meaning, sequence, and context — to transform a product release into an editorial event.

**When to use it:**
- The brand is launching a new collection, seasonal range, or limited release
- A product drop has a specific launch date and needs editorial framing
- The collection has internal logic (a theme, a design progression, a material story) worth articulating
- The release strategy benefits from editorial anticipation and context-setting

**What it is best for:**
- Launching products with editorial weight rather than transactional announcements
- Giving a collection a narrative arc that connects individual pieces
- Building anticipation and understanding before or during a release window
- Positioning the brand's collection decisions as considered, not arbitrary

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The collection has no internal logic — it is just "what's new"
- The latazin lists every item in the collection without editorial selection or hierarchy
- The launch framing is generic ("Introducing our new collection") without specific editorial angle
- The latazin is a press release reformatted as editorial

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Bundle/World**, the grouping is the brand's, not the editor's
- Unlike a **Product Argument**, the focus is on the collection as a whole, not a single product
- Unlike a **Calendar Marking**, the event is a commercial release, not a cultural moment
- Unlike a **Collaboration**, the collection is entirely internal to the brand

---

### 8. Calendar Marking

**What it does:** Acknowledges a moment in time without necessarily selling a product. A Calendar Marking latazin positions the brand as culturally present — aware of seasons, holidays, transitions, and shared experiences. It is the lightest-touch latazin type commercially, and the most important for long-term editorial identity.

**When to use it:**
- A significant cultural, seasonal, or calendar moment deserves acknowledgement
- The brand wants to be present in the reader's life without a transactional motive
- The editorial calendar needs breathing room between commercially driven content
- A moment is too important to ignore but too sensitive for product placement

**What it is best for:**
- Building editorial trust and long-term reader relationship
- Demonstrating cultural awareness and taste
- Creating rhythm in the publishing calendar (not every latazin sells)
- Handling sensitive moments (memorials, cultural observances) with appropriate restraint

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The marking is a thin excuse for a product insertion ("Happy Earth Day! Here's our sustainable range")
- The moment chosen is trivial or invented ("National Sock Day")
- The latazin has nothing substantive to say about the moment
- The cultural marking is tone-deaf or superficial

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike an **Occasion**, a Calendar Marking is not product-forward — products may be absent entirely
- Unlike a **Manifesto**, it marks a moment rather than stating a position
- Unlike a **Community/Project**, it does not centre on people or collective action

---

### 9. Collaboration

**What it does:** Presents a partnership between the brand and an external party — a designer, artist, maker, institution, or another brand. The latazin explains why the collaboration exists, what each party brings, and what the collaboration produces that neither could alone.

**When to use it:**
- The brand has a genuine partnership with an external creative party
- The collaboration produces specific products, experiences, or content
- Both parties bring something the reader can understand and appreciate
- The partnership has a story beyond "two brands put their names on something"

**What it is best for:**
- Expanding the brand's audience through the collaborator's credibility
- Creating content that feels genuinely new and unexpected
- Demonstrating the brand's cultural positioning through the company it keeps
- Generating editorial material with built-in narrative (two perspectives, a process of working together, a shared outcome)

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The collaboration is a licensing deal dressed as editorial content
- The collaborator is not meaningfully introduced — their contribution is vague
- The latazin reads like a joint press release, not an editorial exploration
- The "collaboration" is cosmetic (a logo swap, a colour variant) without substantive creative exchange

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Craft Study**, the focus is on the partnership and combined creation, not internal process
- Unlike a **Collection/Drop**, the editorial angle is the relationship, not the release
- Unlike a **Product Argument**, the collaborator is as important as the product
- Unlike a **Community/Project**, the collaboration is creative/commercial, not community-driven

---

### 10. Shop the Look

**What it does:** Presents a styled, visual-forward composition of products that work together. The editorial logic is demonstration through combination — the reader sees how products relate in use, on a body, in a room, on a table. The writing is leaner than in other types; the image carries much of the argument.

**When to use it:**
- Products are best understood in combination or in situ
- The brand's visual styling is strong enough to carry a latazin with lighter copy
- The reader benefits from seeing products in context rather than isolation
- The editorial calendar needs visual variety after copy-heavy latazins

**What it is best for:**
- Showing (not telling) how products work together
- Driving multi-product awareness through visual storytelling
- Creating content that performs well in visual-first channels
- Providing practical, aspirational styling guidance without instructional tone

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The "look" is just a product grid with no styling logic or editorial eye
- The copy is a list of product names and prices with no contextual glue
- The visual composition is generic (flat lay, white background) without editorial intent
- The latazin is indistinguishable from a catalogue page

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Bundle/World**, a Shop the Look is visual-forward and typically shorter, without the narrative world-building
- Unlike a **Product Argument**, no single product is the focus — the combination is the argument
- Unlike a **Collection/Drop**, the grouping is the stylist's or editor's, and may cross collections or seasons

---

### 11. Community/Project

**What it does:** Centres on people — customers, makers, community members, project participants. The latazin tells a story through or about the people who interact with the brand's products. Products appear as part of real life, not as the subject.

**When to use it:**
- The brand has a genuine community worth showcasing
- Real customers or users have stories that illuminate the brand's values
- A project or initiative connects the brand to its audience in a non-commercial way
- The editorial calendar needs human-centred content to balance product-centred pieces

**What it is best for:**
- Building social proof through real stories, not testimonials
- Demonstrating the brand's community as evidence of its values
- Creating content that feels personal without being promotional
- Giving readers identification points ("people like me use this brand")

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The "community" is a marketing fiction — the people are models, not real users
- The project is trivially connected to the brand
- The latazin uses people as props for product placement
- The community story is generic ("our amazing customers") without specific, named individuals or real narratives

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Collaboration**, the focus is on community, not creative partnership
- Unlike a **Milestone/Proof**, the emphasis is on people's experiences, not the brand's achievements
- Unlike a **Product Argument**, products are incidental to the story, not its subject
- Unlike a **Calendar Marking**, the content is people-centred, not moment-centred

---

### 12. Milestone/Proof

**What it does:** Presents evidence of the brand's track record, achievements, longevity, or impact. A Milestone/Proof latazin makes the case that the brand has earned its position through demonstrated results — not through claims, but through specific, verifiable evidence.

**When to use it:**
- The brand reaches a significant milestone (anniversary, unit count, geographic expansion, cultural recognition)
- Third-party validation provides an editorial opportunity (press coverage, awards, industry recognition)
- Sales data or customer behaviour patterns tell a story worth sharing
- The brand's track record is itself an argument for its products

**What it is best for:**
- Building long-term brand credibility through evidence
- Converting sceptical or new readers through demonstrated proof
- Creating cornerstone content that product-level latazins can reference
- Celebrating real achievements without manufactured self-congratulation

**What weak use cases look like:**
- The "milestone" is minor or irrelevant to the reader ("our 500th Instagram post!")
- The latazin is self-congratulatory without substance
- The proof is vague ("thousands of happy customers") rather than specific
- The milestone is an excuse for a retrospective product dump

**How it differs from related types:**
- Unlike a **Manifesto**, the authority comes from evidence, not argument
- Unlike a **Community/Project**, the focus is on the brand's achievements, not its people
- Unlike a **Product Argument**, the subject is the brand's track record, not a specific product
- Unlike a **Calendar Marking**, the moment is commercial/brand-specific, not cultural

## Type Selection Guidance

### Choosing the Right Type

When selecting a type for a latazin, ask:

1. **What is the editorial question?** Every latazin answers a question. "Why this product?" is a Product Argument. "How is this made?" is a Craft Study. "What do people get wrong?" is a Misreading Correction. If you cannot articulate the question, the latazin does not have a type — it has a product list.

2. **What does the reader gain?** A Product Argument gives the reader a purchasing framework. An Occasion gives temporal relevance. A Craft Study gives knowledge. A Community/Project gives identification. If the reader gains nothing beyond product awareness, the type is probably wrong.

3. **Where does the product sit in the argument?** In a Product Argument, the product is the subject. In an Occasion, the product is the solution. In a Bundle/World, the product is part of an environment. In a Calendar Marking, the product may be absent entirely. The product's position determines the type.

4. **What would make this latazin fail?** If removing the product leaves nothing, it is a weak Product Argument. If removing the occasion leaves nothing, it is a weak Occasion. If the craft detail is generic, the Craft Study fails. The type's failure mode reveals whether the type is correctly chosen.

### Type Decision Table

| Editorial question | Primary type | Consider also |
|---|---|---|
| Why should I buy this specific product? | Product Argument | Craft Study (if process is the answer) |
| What goes with what? | Bundle/World or Shop the Look | Collection/Drop (if brand-defined grouping) |
| How is this made? | Craft Study | Collaboration (if a partner is involved) |
| What should I buy for [occasion]? | Occasion | Bundle/World (if multiple products) |
| What do people misunderstand about this? | Misreading Correction | Manifesto (if the correction is a position) |
| What does this brand believe? | Manifesto | Milestone/Proof (if evidence-driven) |
| What's new right now? | Collection/Drop | Calendar Marking (if moment > product) |
| What's happening in the culture/season? | Calendar Marking | Occasion (if product-forward) |
| Who made this together? | Collaboration | Craft Study (if process is the focus) |
| How would I wear/use/style this? | Shop the Look | Bundle/World (if atmosphere > visual) |
| Who uses this brand? | Community/Project | Milestone/Proof (if evidence > people) |
| Why should I trust this brand? | Milestone/Proof | Community/Project (if proof is social) |

## Type Mixing and Sequencing Notes

### Mixing Types Within a Single Latazin

A latazin has one primary type. However, some latazins incorporate secondary type elements:

- A **Product Argument** might include a Craft Study section as supporting evidence
- A **Collection/Drop** might use Shop the Look pages to show styling within the collection
- An **Occasion** might incorporate Community/Project elements (real people celebrating the occasion)
- A **Collaboration** often includes Craft Study elements (the collaborative process)

**Rules for mixing:**
- The primary type determines the opening, the structural arc, and the closing
- Secondary elements occupy no more than 25–30% of the latazin's page count
- The latazin is filed and reviewed under its primary type
- Mixing more than two types signals that the brief is unfocused

### Sequencing Types Across the Calendar

A healthy editorial calendar uses the full typology. Common sequencing principles:

- **Never publish more than two Product Arguments consecutively.** The reader needs variety. Follow a Product Argument with a Calendar Marking, Occasion, or Community/Project.
- **Place Manifestos and Calendar Markings strategically.** They are breathing room. They belong at the start of seasons, during cultural moments, or when the calendar needs a commercial pause.
- **Pair Craft Studies with Product Arguments.** Publish the Craft Study first, then follow with a Product Argument for a product made through that craft. The study creates context the argument can leverage.
- **Use Collection/Drop sparingly.** Not every product release needs a dedicated latazin. Reserve the type for genuinely significant launches.
- **Ensure at least one Community/Project or Milestone/Proof per quarter.** These types build long-term trust and prevent the editorial calendar from reading as purely commercial.
- **Vary the product density.** Alternate between product-heavy types (Product Argument, Bundle/World, Shop the Look) and product-light types (Calendar Marking, Manifesto, Community/Project).

### Anti-Patterns in Type Usage

- **The all-Product-Argument calendar:** Every latazin sells a single product. The editorial identity collapses into a catalogue.
- **The type-mismatch:** The brief says Craft Study, but the latazin reads as a Product Argument with one paragraph about materials. The type must govern the structure, not just the label.
- **The hybrid trap:** Trying to be a Manifesto, a Product Argument, and a Collaboration simultaneously. Pick one. Support with elements of another, maximum.
- **The forced occasion:** Manufacturing relevance for a product by attaching it to a tenuous calendar moment. If the connection is not genuine, choose a different type.
- **The evidence-free Milestone:** Declaring achievement without specific proof. A Milestone/Proof latazin without numbers, dates, or third-party validation is just a press release.

## Maintenance Notes

Review this file when:

- A new type of commerce content emerges that the existing typology does not cover
- Consistent type mismatches appear in generated content, suggesting type definitions are ambiguous
- Planning documents repeatedly default to the same two or three types, suggesting others are under-defined
- New commerce categories (e.g., digital goods, subscriptions, experiences) enter the system and may need dedicated types or type adaptations
- Reviewer feedback indicates that type boundaries are unclear or that latazins are consistently miscategorised
