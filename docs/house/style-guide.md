# Style Guide

## Purpose

This file defines the sentence-level writing rules that govern every latazin produced by the system. It operates beneath the voice (defined in `house-voice.md`) and above the content structure (defined in `page-role-system.md`). Where the voice document describes *character*, this document describes *mechanics* — how sentences are built, how paragraphs breathe, how openings earn attention, how products are described without becoming catalogue entries, and how price, urgency, and proof are handled with editorial restraint.

A latazin can have the right voice, the right type, the right page structure, and still fail if the writing itself is mechanical, cliché-heavy, rhythmically flat, or tonally inconsistent at the sentence level. This guide prevents that.

---

## How This File Is Used in the System

- **During generation:** The model references this file to constrain sentence construction, paragraph rhythm, opening logic, and product language. It prevents default promotional patterns from appearing in output.
- **During review:** Human reviewers use this file to evaluate whether writing mechanics meet house standards. A latazin may pass the rubric on structure and voice but still require revision if sentence character, rhythm, or CTA handling violate these rules.
- **During planning:** Planning documents do not directly reference this file, but the standards here inform what "quality" means when a plan specifies tone or editorial ambition for a given period.
- **During prompt design:** New prompts incorporate these rules as constraints, ensuring generated content meets mechanical standards without requiring post-generation line editing.

## Sentence Character Rules

Every sentence in a latazin should earn its place. The default state is *removal* — a sentence stays only if it advances the argument, sets atmosphere, or provides necessary detail.

### Principles

- **Prefer declarative sentences.** State what is, not what might be. "The collar sits wide and flat" rather than "You might notice the collar sitting wide and flat."
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** A paragraph of five medium-length sentences is rhythmically dead. Follow a long sentence with a short one. Use a fragment when emphasis demands it.
- **Avoid compound sentences joined by "and" unless both clauses are necessary.** "The leather is vegetable-tanned and the hardware is brass" is two sentences pretending to be one.
- **Front-load meaning.** The most important word or idea in a sentence should appear early, not buried after qualifiers.
- **Eliminate throat-clearing.** Cut "It's worth noting that," "What makes this interesting is," "The thing about," and any construction that delays the point.
- **One idea per sentence.** If a sentence needs "also," "additionally," or "furthermore," it is likely two sentences.
- **Use concrete nouns over abstract ones.** "The stitching" rather than "the craftsmanship." "The weight of the fabric" rather than "the quality."
- **Prefer active voice.** Reserve passive voice for deliberate emphasis shifts ("The lining was chosen for a reason").
- **Avoid rhetorical questions in body copy.** They create false intimacy and weaken authority. State the point directly.

### What to Avoid

- Sentences that begin with "Whether you're" or "If you're looking for"
- Sentences built on unsupported comparisons ("Unlike anything you've seen")
- Sentences that exist only to transition ("Now let's talk about," "Speaking of")
- Sentences that repeat the previous sentence's point in different words

## Tone Rules

Tone operates within the range defined in `house-voice.md` (restrained → composed → warm → inviting). This section specifies how tone manifests at the sentence level.

### Principles

- **Confidence without volume.** A confident sentence does not need an exclamation mark, an intensifier, or a superlative. "This is the coat" is more confident than "This is absolutely the best coat you'll ever own!"
- **Warmth without familiarity.** Address the reader as an intelligent adult. Never condescend, never use "you guys," never assume the reader's emotional state ("You're going to love this").
- **Authority without lecturing.** State positions plainly. Do not explain why the reader should agree. Let the evidence — material, detail, context — carry the argument.
- **Restraint without coldness.** A restrained sentence still has life. "The glaze catches morning light differently than afternoon" is restrained and alive. "The glaze is aesthetically notable" is restrained and dead.
- **Consistency within a piece.** Tone may shift slightly between page roles (a premise page may be more composed, an invitation page more warm), but the shift should be gradual, never jarring.

### Tone Violations

- Starting composed and ending in promotional enthusiasm
- Using casual language ("super," "totally," "honestly") in body copy
- Mixing editorial authority with social media cadence
- Dropping into instructional tone ("Here's how to style it," "Try pairing it with") unless the latazin type specifically calls for it

## Paragraph Rhythm Rules

A latazin is read, not scanned. Paragraph rhythm determines whether the reader stays or leaves.

### Principles

- **Vary paragraph length.** A sequence of three-sentence paragraphs creates monotony. Alternate between longer contextual paragraphs (3–5 sentences) and short emphatic ones (1–2 sentences).
- **One-sentence paragraphs are a tool, not a habit.** Use them for emphasis, pivot, or pause. More than two per page dilutes the effect.
- **The first sentence of a paragraph sets direction.** It should not be a continuation of the previous paragraph's thought — it should open a new angle or advance the argument.
- **The last sentence of a paragraph earns the break.** It should land, not trail off. Avoid ending paragraphs with subordinate clauses or qualifications.
- **White space is editorial.** A paragraph break signals a shift — in time, in angle, in register. Do not break paragraphs arbitrarily for visual comfort.
- **Avoid walls of text.** No paragraph in a latazin should exceed six sentences. If it does, it contains more than one idea.

### Paragraph Sequencing

- Open a section with a grounding paragraph (context, observation, or premise)
- Follow with a development paragraph (detail, evidence, or expansion)
- Close with a landing paragraph (implication, connection, or turn)
- Avoid the pattern of claim → claim → claim. Prefer claim → evidence → implication.

## Opening Logic

How a latazin begins determines whether it earns the reader's attention or loses it within seconds. The opening is not a summary. It is a premise.

### How to Start a Latazin

- **Start with a context the reader recognises.** A season, a moment, a problem, a shift. "November asks a different question of your wardrobe" grounds the reader in shared experience before any product appears.
- **Start with a specific observation.** "Most candles burn in the background. This one occupies the room." The observation creates curiosity.
- **Start with a quiet claim.** "There are only three bags worth owning for summer." A position, stated plainly, invites the reader to stay and hear the argument.
- **Start with an image.** Describe a scene or moment that the latazin will inhabit. "The table is set. Linen, not cotton. Stoneware, not porcelain."
- **Start with a contrast.** "The word 'essential' has been emptied of meaning. This is an attempt to refill it."

### How NOT to Start

- **Do not start with the brand name.** The brand is the context, not the opening.
- **Do not start with a product name.** The product earns its introduction; it does not lead.
- **Do not start with a question.** Rhetorical questions weaken authority ("Ever wondered what makes a great bag?").
- **Do not start with a statistic or data point** unless the latazin type is specifically data-driven.
- **Do not start with "Introducing" or "Meet."** These are catalogue constructions, not editorial ones.
- **Do not start with flattery.** "You deserve," "For the discerning," "Because you value" — these are assumptions, not premises.
- **Do not start with a dictionary definition.** "Webster's defines quality as..." is the hallmark of unearned authority.

## Product Language Rules

Products in a latazin are editorial subjects, not inventory items. They are described in terms of what they do, what they replace, where they belong, and why they matter now — not in terms of features and benefits.

### Principles

- **Name the product clearly, once.** After the first mention, refer to it by a natural shorthand, not the full product name repeated.
- **Describe what the product does, not what it is.** "Keeps its shape through a twelve-hour day" rather than "Made with structured canvas."
- **Place the product in a world.** "This is the jacket you reach for when the forecast says maybe." Context gives the product meaning.
- **Let materials speak through effect.** "The leather softens over months, not years" rather than "Premium full-grain leather."
- **Avoid stacking adjectives.** "Luxurious soft Italian leather" is noise. "Italian leather that softens with wear" is information.
- **Name what the product replaces.** "This replaces the bag you keep rebuying every eighteen months" positions the product in the reader's life.
- **Connect product to time.** Seasons, occasions, life stages, moments of day — products gain meaning from temporal context.
- **Use price as fact, not persuasion.** If price is mentioned, state it. Do not frame it as a deal, a steal, a value, or an investment unless the latazin type specifically builds that argument.

### What to Avoid in Product Language

- Feature lists disguised as prose
- Superlatives without evidence ("the finest," "the best," "unparalleled")
- Emotional projection ("you'll fall in love with," "you'll never want to take it off")
- Technical specifications as selling points (unless the audience and type demand it)
- Comparing the product to competitors by name
- Using "elevated" or "curated" as descriptors

## Cliché Avoidance List

The following words and phrases are prohibited in latazin body copy unless used with deliberate irony, explicit subversion, or in direct quotation. Their presence signals generic commerce writing, not editorial work.

### Banned Phrases

- "Elevate your [anything]"
- "Take your [anything] to the next level"
- "Curated collection"
- "Timeless elegance"
- "Effortlessly chic"
- "Game-changer"
- "Must-have"
- "Treat yourself"
- "You deserve"
- "Drop everything"
- "Obsessed"
- "We're so excited to announce"
- "Just dropped"
- "Limited edition" (state the production quantity instead)
- "Luxury meets [anything]"
- "Redefining [anything]"
- "Designed for the modern [man/woman/person]"
- "Statement piece"
- "Invest in yourself"
- "Self-care essential"
- "The ultimate [anything]"
- "Unlike anything you've seen before"
- "Not your average [anything]"
- "Goes with everything"
- "Wardrobe staple"
- "The perfect gift"

### Banned Constructions

- "Whether you're [scenario A] or [scenario B]" — false inclusivity disguised as relevance
- "From [A] to [B]" as a range of use cases — vague and unearned
- "More than just a [product]" — if it were, you wouldn't need to say so
- "It's not just [X], it's [Y]" — the formula reveals the emptiness
- "[Product] that speaks for itself" — then let it; don't write the sentence

## CTA Restraint Principles

A call to action in a latazin is an invitation, not a command. It is earned by the editorial content that precedes it, not inserted as a conversion mechanism.

### Principles

- **One CTA per latazin, maximum.** If the editorial work is done well, the reader knows what to do. Multiple CTAs signal that the content didn't persuade.
- **Place the CTA at the end, after the argument is complete.** Never mid-content. Never as a pop-up interruption to the reading experience.
- **Use restrained language.** "See the collection" rather than "Shop now!" "View the full range" rather than "Don't miss out!"
- **Never use urgency in a CTA.** No countdowns, no "only X left," no "selling fast." These are catalogue tactics.
- **The CTA should feel like a natural next step,** not a sales pitch. It follows the logic of the content: "The coat is here" after a latazin about the coat.
- **If the latazin is strong enough, the CTA is optional.** Some latazins build atmosphere, brand world, or editorial identity without directing the reader to a specific action. That is valid.

### CTA Anti-Patterns

- "Buy now before it's gone"
- "Add to cart" as editorial language
- Multiple "shop" buttons throughout the piece
- CTAs that contradict the tone of the preceding content
- CTAs that introduce products not discussed in the latazin

## Handling of Price, Urgency, and Proof

### Price

- **State price as fact.** "£185" is a fact. "Only £185" is persuasion. "An incredible value at £185" is noise.
- **Price context is editorial.** "At this price point, the construction is unusual" — this contextualises without selling.
- **Do not use price as a hook.** Never lead with price. Never use price in headlines. Price appears when the product has already earned attention.
- **For ranges, be specific.** "Pieces in this collection range from £45 to £320" is useful. "Affordable luxury" is not.
- **Never use strikethrough pricing, percentage-off language, or sale framing** in editorial latazin content. Sales have their own format and are not editorial.

### Urgency

- **Manufactured urgency is prohibited.** "Only 12 left" and "Ends midnight" are catalogue tools, not editorial ones.
- **Real urgency is acceptable when stated factually.** "This is a single production run of 200 units" is information. "Hurry before they sell out!" is manipulation.
- **Seasonal relevance is not urgency.** "This coat is designed for November" is temporal context, not a countdown.
- **Let the calendar create urgency naturally.** A latazin published in early December about gift-worthy objects carries inherent timeliness. It does not need to manufacture more.

### Proof

- **Proof is demonstrated, not claimed.** "Sold out three times since launch" is proof. "Our bestseller" is a claim.
- **Specific numbers are stronger than adjectives.** "4,200 units sold in its first week" outperforms "incredibly popular."
- **Customer language can be proof when quoted directly.** Do not paraphrase or editorialize customer feedback. Quote it, attribute it, let it stand.
- **Awards, press mentions, and third-party recognition are proof when cited specifically.** "Selected by [Publication]" is proof. "Award-winning" without citation is a claim.
- **Do not stack proof.** One or two proof points, placed naturally within the editorial flow, are more credible than a list of testimonials.
- **Proof appears in the argument, not the opening.** The latazin earns the reader's attention with premise and context. Proof reinforces, it does not lead.

## Formatting Conventions

### Text Formatting

- **Headlines:** Sentence case. No all-caps. No exclamation marks. Headlines should be specific enough to distinguish this latazin from any other.
- **Subheadings:** Used sparingly. A latazin is not a blog post with H2 signposts. If subheadings are used, they should advance the argument, not summarise the section.
- **Bold text:** Reserved for product names on first mention and for structural emphasis in longer latazins. Not for emotional emphasis.
- **Italic text:** Reserved for publication titles, foreign words on first use, and deliberate tonal shifts. Not for emphasis.
- **Exclamation marks:** Prohibited in body copy. Permitted only in direct quotations.
- **Ellipses:** Prohibited. They signal trailing thought, not editorial precision.
- **Em dashes:** Used for parenthetical insertions and abrupt shifts. Maximum two per paragraph.
- **Lists:** Avoided in body copy. If a latazin requires a list, it should be a design element, not a prose shortcut. Exceptions exist for specific latazin types (e.g., Shop the Look).

### Length

- **Sentence length:** Vary between 8 and 30 words. Average should sit around 15–18 words.
- **Paragraph length:** 1–6 sentences. Average should sit around 3.
- **Latazin length:** Determined by type (see `commerce-latazin-typology.md`). No latazin should be longer than it needs to be to make its argument.

### Numbers and Measurements

- Spell out numbers one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above.
- Always use numerals for prices, measurements, and quantities.
- Use the brand's local currency symbol. No conversion notes.
- Spell out percentages in body copy ("twelve per cent"). Use the symbol in data contexts ("12%").
