# Campaign Pattern Library

## Purpose

This is a living library of proven editorial campaign patterns — repeatable structures that have worked across brands, seasons, and product categories within the Latazin Editorial Operating System. Each pattern documents a way of combining latazin types, products, occasions, and timing into a coherent campaign arc.

Patterns are not prescriptions. They are structural blueprints that a team can adapt to their brand, products, and audience. The library grows as brands discover and refine new patterns through practice.

---

## How This File Is Used in the System

This file supports the planning layer. Consult it when:

- **Building a quarterly plan** — to identify which campaign patterns fit the quarter's objectives, products, and occasions.
- **Filling a monthly calendar** — to structure a multi-latazin sequence around a launch, occasion, or theme.
- **Writing a latazin brief** — to understand where a single latazin sits within a larger campaign arc.
- **Reviewing performance** — to assess whether a pattern delivered as expected and whether it should be reused, refined, or retired.

## Patterns by Latazin Type

### The Product Build-Up

**Category:** Product Argument
**When to use:** When a single hero product needs sustained editorial attention — typically a new release, a signature piece, or a product being repositioned.
**What it looks like:** Three latazins over 2–3 weeks that approach the same product from different angles, building understanding and desire without repetition.
**Why it works:** Readers encounter the product multiple times through different editorial lenses. Each latazin adds a layer of meaning. By the third encounter, the product feels familiar and understood rather than merely promoted.

**Structure:**

| Sequence | Latazin type | Role | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Craft Study | Introduce the product through its making — materials, process, decisions | Week 1 |
| 2 | Product Argument | Make the case — why this product, why now, what it replaces | Week 2 |
| 3 | Shop the Look or Bundle / World | Show the product in context — pairings, styling, the world it belongs to | Week 3 |

**Variations:**
- For premium products, lead with Manifesto (brand position) before Craft Study
- For seasonal products, replace Shop the Look with Occasion (tie to seasonal moment)
- For restocks, compress to two latazins (Product Argument + Shop the Look)

**Watch out for:**
- Each latazin must stand alone — a reader encountering only one should still gain value
- Avoid repeating the same product details across all three pieces
- The sequence should feel like deepening, not repeating

### The Collection Launch Arc

**Category:** Collection / Drop
**When to use:** When a new collection or capsule is launching and requires coordinated editorial coverage spanning pre-launch through post-launch.
**What it looks like:** Four to five latazins over 3–4 weeks that build anticipation, introduce the collection, explore individual pieces, and sustain interest after the initial announcement.
**Why it works:** A launch is an editorial event, not a single announcement. This pattern gives the collection room to breathe and gives different audience segments different entry points.

**Structure:**

| Sequence | Latazin type | Role | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Manifesto or Craft Study | Set the stage — the idea behind the collection, the making | 1–2 weeks pre-launch |
| 2 | Collection / Drop | The launch piece — the full collection presented editorially | Launch day / week |
| 3 | Product Argument | Deep dive into the hero product from the collection | Week after launch |
| 4 | Shop the Look or Occasion | Show collection pieces in context — styled, in use, for a moment | 2 weeks after launch |
| 5 | Community / Project (optional) | Feature early adopters or the people behind the collection | 3 weeks after launch |

**Variations:**
- For capsule collections (fewer pieces), compress to three latazins
- For collaborative collections, replace Manifesto with Collaboration as the opener
- For limited editions, add urgency through specificity ("Single run of 200 units") not through manufactured pressure

**Watch out for:**
- The launch piece (sequence 2) should not try to do everything — it introduces, it does not argue every product
- Post-launch latazins should add new information, not recap the launch
- Avoid letting the arc feel like a countdown or marketing funnel

## Patterns by Occasion

### The Gift-Giving Arc

**Category:** Occasion (gift-driven)
**When to use:** When a major gift-giving occasion approaches — Mother's Day, Father's Day, Valentine's Day, holiday season. Works best with 10–21 days of lead time.
**What it looks like:** Two to three latazins that reframe gifting as an act of selection and intention rather than obligation.
**Why it works:** Gift-giving occasions are commercially important but editorially dangerous — they invite generic, pressured content. This pattern earns the right to sell by first establishing the editorial idea that a good gift reflects judgment, not budget.

**Structure:**

| Sequence | Latazin type | Role | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Occasion or Calendar Marking | Set the tone — what this occasion means beyond commerce | 14–21 days before occasion |
| 2 | Bundle / World | Present a curated selection — products grouped by recipient type, price band, or aesthetic | 7–14 days before occasion |
| 3 | Product Argument (optional) | Spotlight one standout gift with a full editorial treatment | 5–7 days before occasion |

**Variations:**
- For smaller occasions (birthdays, thank-yous), a single Occasion latazin may suffice
- For holiday season (November–December), expand to 4–6 latazins across the full period
- For audiences resistant to gift guides, lead with Misreading Correction ("Why most gift guides fail")

**Watch out for:**
- Avoid "treat her/him" language — the house voice demands more specificity
- Gift guides must demonstrate taste through selection, not just list products
- Timing is critical — publish too early and it feels premature; too late and it is useless

## Patterns by Product Category

### The Everyday Carry Pattern

**Category:** Bags, accessories, daily-use items
**When to use:** When featuring products positioned as daily companions — bags, wallets, folios, key holders, phone cases. Works especially well during back-to-work transitions (January, September).
**What it looks like:** Two latazins that position a product as an upgrade to daily routine, not a luxury purchase.
**Why it works:** Everyday products compete against inertia ("my current bag is fine"). This pattern overcomes inertia by making the reader notice what their current routine lacks.

**Structure:**

| Sequence | Latazin type | Role | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Misreading Correction or Contrast-led Product Argument | Challenge the assumption that daily items don't matter — name what most people settle for and why settling costs more than upgrading | First |
| 2 | Product Argument | Present the product as the resolution — material detail, daily-use positioning, what changes | 3–5 days later |

**Variations:**
- Add a Shop the Look as a third piece to show the product alongside daily companions
- For premium daily-use items, add Craft Study to justify the investment
- For seasonal transitions, frame through Occasion ("The September reset")

**Watch out for:**
- Avoid lifestyle aspiration language — keep it grounded in material and practical detail
- "Everyday" positioning should feel honest, not forced ("this bag goes with everything" is a banned construction)

## Patterns by Season

### The Seasonal Transition Pattern

**Category:** Seasonal (any transition: winter→spring, summer→autumn, etc.)
**When to use:** When the calendar crosses a seasonal boundary and products, mood, and reader behaviour shift. Most powerful during early autumn and early spring when change is tangible.
**What it looks like:** Two to three latazins that acknowledge the shift and present products as appropriate to the emerging season — not the one being left behind.
**Why it works:** Seasonal transitions create natural editorial energy. Readers are already thinking about what changes. This pattern meets them there with specificity and restraint.

**Structure:**

| Sequence | Latazin type | Role | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Calendar Marking or Atmosphere-led Occasion | Mark the transition — describe what is changing (light, temperature, rhythm, wardrobe) | First week of transition |
| 2 | Product Argument or Bundle / World | Present products that belong to the new season — why these materials, colours, weights, textures now | Mid-transition |
| 3 | Shop the Look (optional) | Show seasonal styling — the visual argument for the new season's palette and texture | Late transition |

**Variations:**
- For brands with strong seasonal collections, tie sequence 2 to a Collection / Drop
- For brands with year-round products, focus on repositioning existing products for the new season
- For tropical or non-seasonal climates, replace weather cues with cultural or social rhythm cues

**Watch out for:**
- Don't rush the season — "autumn in August" alienates readers still in summer
- Seasonal content should be sensory and specific, not generic ("cosy vibes" is banned territory)
- The Calendar Marking opener should stand on its own editorially, even if no products were mentioned

## How to Add New Patterns

When a campaign performs well and its structure seems repeatable, document it here using the pattern entry format above. Follow these steps:

1. **Identify the repeatable structure.** What types were used, in what order, at what cadence? Strip away the brand-specific details and describe the skeleton.
2. **Name the pattern.** Use a clear, descriptive name that another team could understand without context.
3. **Categorise it.** Place it under the most relevant heading (by type, occasion, product category, or season). If it fits multiple categories, place it under the primary one and note the others.
4. **Document the logic.** Explain *why* it works, not just *what* it is. The "why" is what makes it transferable.
5. **Note variations and failure modes.** A pattern without caveats is a prescription, not a tool.
6. **Reference the performance data.** If possible, note which brand and quarter first used this pattern and link to the relevant performance log entry.
