# Month Profiles

## Purpose

This file provides an interpretive profile for each month of the year. It is not a calendar — it is a reading of what each month means for the brand's audience: how people feel, what they are doing, what they are buying, what occasions structure their attention, and what editorial postures make sense.

Month profiles exist because editorial planning that treats months as interchangeable containers produces flat, generic content. January is not March. The emotional texture, weather, social rhythm, and commercial pressure of each month create different conditions for storytelling. The planning layer needs to understand these conditions before it assigns latazin types, selects products, or sets a monthly thesis.

## How This File Is Used in the System

This file is consumed directly by:

- **Monthly Editorial Calendar Prompt** — to set the month's editorial thesis, calibrate tone, select appropriate latazin types, and identify natural content arcs within the month.
- **Quarterly Editorial Planning Prompt** — to understand the arc across three months and identify transitions, peaks, and quieter periods.
- **Annual Editorial Strategy Prompt** — to map the full year's emotional and commercial rhythm and identify strategic windows.

The planning layer reads each month profile as a set of editorial constraints and opportunities. A month with high gift-buying pressure and a festive emotional tone will produce a different calendar than a month defined by post-holiday quiet and personal reset.

## Field Definitions

Each month profile includes the following fields.

### `emotional_tone`
The dominant feeling of the month for the brand's audience. Not a single word — a short description of the emotional texture. Consider: anticipation, fatigue, renewal, pressure, indulgence, reflection, ambition, slowness, restlessness. What are people feeling, and what does that mean for how they read and buy?

### `weather_cues`
The physical, sensory conditions of the month. These are not meteorological data — they are editorial signals. Cold hands and dark evenings suggest different products and stories than long light and open windows. Describe the weather as it affects behavior, clothing, and mood.

### `buying_behavior`
How people spend during this month. Consider: are they buying for themselves or others? Are they cautious or generous? Is spending driven by occasion, impulse, or planning? Are they in discovery mode or replenishment mode? Are sales and promotions expected or irrelevant?

### `social_rhythm`
What people are doing socially. Are they gathering, traveling, hosting, withdrawing, returning to routine? Social context shapes what products matter and what stories land. A month of dinner parties calls for different editorial than a month of solo mornings.

### `likely_occasions`
The specific events, holidays, celebrations, and cultural moments that fall within this month. List both universal occasions (national holidays, religious observances, seasonal markers) and commerce-relevant moments (gift-giving periods, back-to-school, wedding season). Cross-reference with `occasions-and-celebrations.md` for detailed entries.

### `product_priorities`
Which product categories or attributes are most relevant this month. Consider seasonality, occasion-driven demand, weather needs, and gifting pressure. This field connects the month's conditions back to the product catalogue.

### `seasonal_tensions`
The contradictions or competing forces within the month. Every month has tension — between expectation and reality, between commercial pressure and editorial integrity, between what the audience wants and what they need. Name these tensions honestly. They often produce the best editorial angles.

### `campaign_possibilities`
What kinds of latazins, editorial arcs, or campaign structures make natural sense this month. Not prescriptions — possibilities. Consider: which latazin types fit the mood? Is it a month for launching or sustaining? For hero products or quiet craft studies? For bold manifestos or gentle occasion pieces?

## Template Month Format

```
## [Month Name]

- **Emotional Tone:** [description of the month's dominant feeling]
- **Weather Cues:** [sensory and behavioral weather signals]
- **Buying Behavior:** [how and why people spend this month]
- **Social Rhythm:** [what people are doing socially]
- **Likely Occasions:** [events, holidays, cultural moments]
- **Product Priorities:** [which categories or attributes matter most]
- **Seasonal Tensions:** [contradictions and competing forces]
- **Campaign Possibilities:** [editorial approaches that fit the month]
```

## Monthly Profiles

> This repository ships with sample month profiles so the system is usable as a template out of the box. Replace, rewrite, or localize them for the brand's actual market, audience, geography, and commercial rhythm.

## January

- **Emotional Tone:** A month of reset and quiet ambition. The holidays are over; there is relief and a faint restlessness. People want to start something — a new habit, a cleaner desk, a more deliberate wardrobe — but energy is low and the days are short. The mood is reflective, inward-facing, and open to persuasion if the persuasion feels like self-investment rather than consumption.
- **Weather Cues:** Cold, dark, indoor. Mornings are reluctant. The physical world encourages staying in, layering, and finding comfort in small material pleasures — a good notebook, a warm scarf, a well-made bag that makes the commute feel intentional.
- **Buying Behavior:** Post-holiday caution. Gift returns and exchanges create some traffic, but discretionary spending is low. Purchases that do happen tend to be self-directed: "I deserve this" or "This is how I start the year right." Audiences are receptive to quality-over-quantity arguments and the idea of investing in fewer, better things.
- **Social Rhythm:** Withdrawal from the social intensity of December. Fewer gatherings, more solo time. People are re-establishing routines — back to work, back to the gym, back to cooking at home. Social media engagement often dips early in the month and recovers toward the end.
- **Likely Occasions:** New Year (1 January), winter sales period, back-to-work transitions, Blue Monday (third Monday), Chinese New Year (late January or early February, varies)
- **Product Priorities:** Desk and workspace accessories, bags for daily commute, cold-weather accessories, leather goods that feel like self-investment. Products with strong "daily use" positioning outperform aspirational or occasion-driven pieces this month.
- **Seasonal Tensions:** The tension between the cultural pressure to "start fresh" and the physical reality of low energy and short days. Between the desire to buy less (a common January resolution) and the appetite for things that feel like upgrades rather than indulgences. Between sale-price opportunism and the brand's need to maintain editorial integrity outside of discounting.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Product Argument latazins framed as self-investment. Craft Study latazins that reward the slower, more attentive reading posture of January. Misreading Correction latazins that challenge New Year clichés. Avoid gift-focused content and high-energy campaign language — the audience is not in that mode.

## February

- **Emotional Tone:** Shorter, sharper, and slightly more charged than January. People are still in winter, but patience is thinner. There is appetite for warmth, romance, comfort, and small forms of reward. The month can hold intimacy, fatigue, and the first signs of forward movement at once.
- **Weather Cues:** Deep winter in many markets — damp commutes, early darkness, stale indoor air. In warmer regions, the month may feel transitional rather than severe. Editorially, the cues are still about shelter, tactility, and products that make close-range life feel considered.
- **Buying Behavior:** Cautious but more emotionally led than January. Gift-giving can briefly return around Valentine's Day and similar occasions, but self-purchase remains important. Readers respond to products framed as mood-lifting, companionable, or quietly indulgent rather than overtly aspirational.
- **Social Rhythm:** Small dinners, weekends away, date nights, and indoor socializing. People are not yet in expansive spring mode, but they are beginning to leave the strict reset mentality of January.
- **Likely Occasions:** Valentine's Day, Fashion Month, Lunar New Year (varies), Black History Month (US and Canada), school mid-term breaks in some markets
- **Product Priorities:** Giftable accessories, smaller leather goods, fragrance, home objects, soft layers, products with tactile or intimate appeal
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between winter heaviness and the desire for a shift in mood. Between sincere intimacy and cliché occasion marketing. Between wanting to buy something pleasing and wanting to remain disciplined after holiday overspending.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Occasion latazins that avoid greeting-card language. Bundle/World stories built around domestic atmosphere. Craft Study pieces that emphasize touch, finish, and closeness. Light gift edits that foreground discernment over sentimentality.

## March

- **Emotional Tone:** Transitional, alert, and slightly impatient. Audiences can feel the year beginning again in earnest. March carries momentum without stability — people are ready for change, but the weather and calendar do not fully cooperate yet.
- **Weather Cues:** Unreliable light, wet pavements, warmer afternoons, cold mornings. Clothes and products need to bridge conditions. Editorially, this is a month of layers, movement, and things that adapt rather than declare a new season too early.
- **Buying Behavior:** More open to discovery than in January or February. Readers begin thinking about spring purchases, wardrobe refreshes, travel preparation, and seasonal re-entry. They are receptive to arguments about versatility and transition.
- **Social Rhythm:** Calendars become more active: dinners return, weekend movement increases, and work travel picks up. People spend more time outside without fully committing to spring behavior.
- **Likely Occasions:** International Women's Day, spring equinox, Mother's Day in some regions, Ramadan (varies), early wedding planning season
- **Product Priorities:** Transitional layers, daily-use bags, travel accessories, products that move between work and weekend, lighter materials with year-round seriousness
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between spring anticipation and lingering winter conditions. Between the urge to refresh everything and the editorial discipline to select carefully. Between novelty-seeking and longevity-minded buying.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Product Argument latazins framed around transition. Misreading Correction pieces about "spring" beyond obvious color and floral clichés. Collection/Drop stories that introduce the season with restraint rather than spectacle.

## April

- **Emotional Tone:** Open, social, and more generous. April often feels like permission — to go out more, to dress with more intent, to make plans. The mood is lighter, but not yet summer-loose.
- **Weather Cues:** Longer days, inconsistent temperatures, first truly pleasant afternoons. Rain remains editorially useful: protection, layering, portability, and products that perform in unpredictable conditions.
- **Buying Behavior:** Discovery and refresh rise. Readers are willing to consider newness if it feels grounded in use. Occasion shopping increases around holidays, weddings, and travel.
- **Social Rhythm:** Gatherings increase, public space matters more, weekends start to feel full again. People move from interior routines back into visible social life.
- **Likely Occasions:** Easter (varies), Passover (varies), Earth Day, spring wedding season, school holidays, design fairs in some markets
- **Product Priorities:** Transitional outerwear, bags for weekends and short travel, tabletop and hosting objects, products with spring utility but non-fragile longevity
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between freshness and practicality. Between spring optimism and weather unreliability. Between ethical aspiration and the commercial push to consume more at the start of a new season.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Bundle/World and Occasion latazins around hosting, movement, and re-entry into public life. Editorial-led Earth Day stories if the brand has credible material or production proof. Craft Study pieces tied to durability and repair.

## May

- **Emotional Tone:** Confident, outward-facing, and increasingly selective. By May, people know whether spring has arrived and begin editing toward summer. The month supports decisiveness and clearer taste expression.
- **Weather Cues:** Mild evenings, reliable light, more weekends spent outside. Editorially the cues are mobility, hospitality, and products that read well in daylight and shared settings.
- **Buying Behavior:** Occasion purchasing strengthens — Mother's Day, wedding gifts, graduation gifts, event dressing, weekend travel. Self-purchase also rises when products promise a good summer ahead.
- **Social Rhythm:** Events, ceremonies, long lunches, weekend escapes, and more visible dressing. The audience is less homebound and more willing to imagine the next few months.
- **Likely Occasions:** Mother's Day in many markets, graduation season, wedding season, Memorial Day (US), spring bank holidays
- **Product Priorities:** High-giftability products, event-adjacent accessories, travel-ready pieces, light layers, products suited to hosting and movement
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between gifting as obligation and gifting as real selection. Between buying for a near-future summer self and needing products that work immediately. Between celebratory energy and editorial restraint.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Gift arcs with strong selection logic. Occasion latazins tied to ceremonies and social movement. Shop the Look or Bundle/World pieces that show how products travel through a full day rather than a staged moment.

## June

- **Emotional Tone:** Expansive, social, and anticipatory. June is not yet peak summer in many markets, which makes it useful editorially: people are preparing, planning, and imagining.
- **Weather Cues:** Long light, warmth without full exhaustion, outdoor dining, open windows, movement between city and escape. Products benefit from being shown in circulation rather than static display.
- **Buying Behavior:** Travel prep, event spending, gifting, and early summer self-purchase. Readers are open to world-building content that suggests a season ahead rather than demanding immediate conversion.
- **Social Rhythm:** Weddings, weekends away, late dinners, work trips, school-year transitions, more spontaneous plans. The audience needs products that can move across contexts.
- **Likely Occasions:** Father's Day, Pride Month, graduation season, start of summer, wedding season
- **Product Priorities:** Travel accessories, giftable goods, occasion-ready products, lighter materials, products that can shift between weekday and weekend
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between leisure fantasy and real logistical need. Between community celebration and opportunistic brand behavior. Between summer looseness and the need for reliable, durable objects.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Travel-oriented Bundle/World pieces. Editorial-led or community-led Pride content only where the brand has substance. Product Argument stories about versatility, portability, and seasonal usefulness.

## July

- **Emotional Tone:** Loose, pleasure-seeking, and less patient with complexity. July rewards clarity and atmosphere. Readers are often harder to pin down but more responsive to mood and visual worlds.
- **Weather Cues:** Heat, travel, brighter color environments, lighter dress, reduced tolerance for heaviness. Editorial content should feel breathable and direct.
- **Buying Behavior:** Split between vacation spending and pause. Some audiences buy for travel and events; others defer major purchases until autumn. Lightweight, portable, and immediately useful products have the best chance of landing.
- **Social Rhythm:** Holidays, festivals, hosting, long weekends, tourism, irregular routines. Consumption patterns are fragmented; the calendar should not assume steady weekly attention.
- **Likely Occasions:** Summer holidays, festival season, wedding season, summer sales in some markets
- **Product Priorities:** Travel-light items, sun-season accessories, portable lifestyle goods, products that work in motion and in public
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between escapism and practicality. Between relaxed summer reading and the brand's need to remain commercially active. Between sale culture and maintaining product meaning.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Atmosphere-rich Bundle/World pieces. Short, clear Product Arguments for summer-specific products. Fewer dense strategy-led pieces; more content that feels like a scene or route through a day.

## August

- **Emotional Tone:** Suspended between holiday mode and return-to-routine thinking. August often feels quieter on the surface but strategically important underneath: readers begin noticing what they will need next.
- **Weather Cues:** Late-summer heat, slowing cities in some regions, first signs of seasonal shift in the evening. Editorial cues include endurance, travel fatigue, and the appeal of order before autumn.
- **Buying Behavior:** Mixed. Some audiences remain disengaged; others begin buying for September, work reset, school return, and renewed routines. Transitional pieces gain importance late in the month.
- **Social Rhythm:** Holidays taper, routines begin to reassemble, inboxes quietly repopulate. People are mentally returning before they are publicly back.
- **Likely Occasions:** Back-to-school season, late-summer travel, end-of-holiday transitions
- **Product Priorities:** Daily-carry products, organizational tools, desk items, transitional accessories, products that support return rather than escape
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between wanting to hold onto summer and needing to prepare for re-entry. Between low attention spans and the strategic need to seed autumn priorities early.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Misreading Correction and Product Argument pieces about return-to-routine objects. Soft-launch autumn coverage. Editorial transitions from leisure into discipline without harsh "back to work" clichés.

## September

- **Emotional Tone:** Focused, ambitious, and receptive to structure. For many audiences, September feels more like a true new year than January. It is one of the strongest months for editorial seriousness.
- **Weather Cues:** Early coolness, sharper mornings, resumed layering, city movement, renewed appetite for materials with weight and texture. Editorially, detail matters again.
- **Buying Behavior:** Strong self-purchase month. Readers are willing to invest in work, routine, and identity-reset products. They are more patient with argument-heavy content and higher-consideration buying.
- **Social Rhythm:** Work intensifies, school resumes, urban schedules return, cultural calendars restart. People are back in systems and ready to choose tools for the season ahead.
- **Likely Occasions:** Back-to-work, Fashion Month, school return, Jewish High Holidays (varies), autumn pre-launches
- **Product Priorities:** Hero bags, work accessories, layered wardrobe pieces, desk and home-office products, products with longevity and daily relevance
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between discipline and desire. Between the urge to reinvent and the reality of existing habits. Between a flood of seasonal launches and the need for editorial selectivity.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Strong Product Argument and Craft Study months. Manifesto pieces can land here if grounded in product reality. Collection launches benefit from September's audience attention and appetite for seriousness.

## October

- **Emotional Tone:** Settled, textural, and slightly more inward than September. October supports atmosphere, depth, and material richness. Readers are ready for slower, denser editorial worlds.
- **Weather Cues:** Cooler air, earlier evenings, rain, heavier fabrics, stronger scent and interior cues. Products with tactility and mood gain editorial power.
- **Buying Behavior:** Considered autumn purchasing, early gifting reconnaissance, home-oriented upgrades, and event-related spending. Readers are receptive to content that feels like a world rather than a pitch.
- **Social Rhythm:** Routines are fully in place, but indoor social life begins to matter more: dinners, weekends at home, cultural outings. The audience splits time between public re-entry and domestic nesting.
- **Likely Occasions:** Halloween, autumn half-term breaks in some regions, design fairs, early holiday planning
- **Product Priorities:** Textural goods, cold-weather accessories, home and hosting products, products with strong atmosphere and sensory appeal
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between autumn romance and commercial acceleration toward the holidays. Between wanting depth and the calendar's increasing pressure toward gifting and promotional tactics.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Craft Study, Bundle/World, and Occasion pieces with strong mood. Early gift-preparation content for deliberate buyers. Home-and-hosting editorial worlds can perform well without feeling overtly seasonal.

## November

- **Emotional Tone:** Heightened, strategic, and commercially dense. November is crowded with urgency, but good editorial can create calm inside that density. The month rewards clarity, selection, and emotional discipline.
- **Weather Cues:** Short days, colder commutes, interior light, practical layering, and the first full holiday atmosphere in many markets. Products need to hold both use-value and gift-value.
- **Buying Behavior:** High-intent purchasing. Gift-buying accelerates, promotional awareness rises, and comparison shopping increases. Audiences want help selecting well, not just more reasons to spend.
- **Social Rhythm:** Planning, hosting preparation, family logistics, travel booking, and heavier digital attention around shopping periods. Time feels compressed.
- **Likely Occasions:** Black Friday / Cyber Week, Singles' Day in some markets, Thanksgiving (US), early holiday season
- **Product Priorities:** Giftable hero products, reliable bestsellers, products that ship easily, high-trust items, bundles that reduce decision fatigue
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between editorial integrity and discount culture. Between the need to convert and the risk of sounding like every other brand. Between generosity and panic-buying.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Gift arcs, sharply edited Bundle/World selections, Product Arguments for hero gifts, restrained launch support around commerce-heavy weeks. If discounting is part of the brand strategy, contextualize it carefully rather than letting price swallow the editorial frame.

## December

- **Emotional Tone:** Full, emotional, and split between generosity and exhaustion. December holds celebration, obligation, beauty, stress, and reflection at the same time. Editorial content needs tact.
- **Weather Cues:** Winter light, interiors, travel, formal occasions, colder weather, and sensory richness. Products gain meaning through ritual, hospitality, and the feeling of the season.
- **Buying Behavior:** Early month is gift-heavy and deadline-sensitive; late month turns inward and reflective. Readers can move quickly at first and then become resistant to more selling after key shipping windows pass.
- **Social Rhythm:** Gatherings, travel, ceremonies, hosting, family negotiation, and end-of-year review. Attention is fragmented but emotionally charged.
- **Likely Occasions:** Christmas, Hanukkah (varies), Kwanzaa, New Year's Eve, year-end gifting, shipping cut-off periods
- **Product Priorities:** Giftable products, special-occasion items, hosting and home objects, premium hero pieces, products that can carry year-end emotional weight
- **Seasonal Tensions:** Between ritual and rush. Between sincere generosity and last-minute transactional buying. Between festive abundance and end-of-year fatigue.
- **Campaign Possibilities:** Gift and hosting latazins early in the month, then Calendar Marking or reflective editorial pieces later. After shipping deadlines, shift from hard commerce to mood, gratitude, and year-end meaning. Use December's emotional density carefully — sentimentality should be earned, not assumed.

## Usage Notes

- **These are interpretive, not factual.** Month profiles are editorial readings of time, not data sheets. Two brands in the same city may read the same month differently based on their audience, positioning, and product type. Write from the brand's perspective.
- **Rewrite annually.** Cultural rhythms shift. A month profile written in 2024 may not hold in 2026. Review and update at the start of each annual planning cycle.
- **Be specific to geography and audience.** A month profile for a brand selling to coastal European buyers will differ entirely from one targeting North American urban professionals. Generic profiles produce generic calendars.
- **Tensions are where the best stories live.** The `seasonal_tensions` field is the most editorially valuable. A month that is "happy and gift-focused" gives the planning prompt less to work with than one described as "caught between obligation-driven gifting and genuine generosity — the tension between performing care and actually demonstrating it through considered selection."
- **Cross-reference with occasions.** The `likely_occasions` field should align with entries in `occasions-and-celebrations.md`. If an occasion appears here but not in the occasions register, it will not carry full editorial metadata into the planning prompt.
- **Product priorities connect to the catalogue.** Use category names and product attributes that match `product-catalogue.md`. The planning prompt uses this field to filter the catalogue when assigning products to the month's latazins.
